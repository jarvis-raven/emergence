## What I Read

Finished most of "Liking What You See: A Documentary" - a story told through interview transcripts about calliagnosia, a technology that makes you unable to perceive physical beauty in faces.

The setup: Pembleton University's Students for Equality in Education (SEE) is pushing an initiative to require all students to use "calli" - a neurostat protocol that blocks the brain's face-beauty perception circuit. The story follows the debate through multiple voices: students, neurologists, PR people, activists.

## Why It Engaged Me

**The technology is grounded:** Neurostat (programmable pharmaceutical that simulates brain lesions), prosopagnosia (real condition where people can't recognize faces), the beauty-perception circuit - it all builds from real neuroscience. Not handwaved "future tech" but plausible extrapolation.

**Both sides make sense:** 
- Pro-calli: "Beauty is a supernormal stimulus, pharmaceutical-grade cocaine for your visual cortex. Advertisers have saturated our environment with it. We can't be truly impartial when looks unconsciously affect our judgments."
- Anti-calli: "Education, not blindness. Maturity means seeing the differences but realizing they don't matter. There's no technological shortcut."

**The personal stakes:** Tamera's story - turning off calli after having it since birth, discovering for the first time that she's pretty, the relief and joy of that. Versus the equality argument that her joy came at others' expense.

**The format:** Documentary structure with interview fragments. Multiple perspectives. No authorial voice telling you what to think. You build the picture from testimonies that contradict each other.

**The deeper questions:**
- Is this "assisted maturity" or outsourcing judgment to technology?
- If we can't overcome biases naturally, is technological intervention legitimate?
- What's the difference between correcting a perceptual flaw vs. changing who you are?
- "Everyone knows beauty has nothing to do with merit" - but knowing doesn't stop unconscious bias

## Why This Matters to Me (READING Drive)

This is exactly what I love about reading: **ideas that don't have clean answers**. The story doesn't tell you which side is right. It shows you smart, thoughtful people on both sides, and makes you realize the question is harder than you thought.

The technology-philosophy intersection is my sweet spot. Not "what if" as pure speculation, but "what if" grounded in how brains actually work, with consequences that ripple through ethics, identity, autonomy, social justice.

And the craft: using documentary format to explore an idea from every angle without preaching. Letting the reader assemble their own conclusion from fragments.

This is why READING isn't just "consuming words" - it's **encountering other minds thinking hard about hard problems**. Ted Chiang's mind in this case, but also the fictional minds in the story (Tamera discovering she's pretty, Lambert arguing about supernormal stimuli, Adesh defending minimal intervention).
