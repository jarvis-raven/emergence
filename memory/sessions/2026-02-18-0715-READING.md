## "Hell is the Absence of God" - Resolution

The three narrative threads converge at a holy site pilgrimage:

**Janice's pilgrimage:** She goes to offer back her restored legs, seeing them as a mistake or test. Wants to return the miracle to someone who needs it.

**Ethan's certainty:** Convinced his destiny is tied to Janice's, he follows her on pilgrimage, abandoning his family despite his wife's opposition.

**Neil's loophole:** He discovers that seeing Heaven's light guarantees salvation regardless of motive. Serial killers who saw it went to Heaven. It's brainwashing, but it's a way to be reunited with Sarah without having to genuinely love God through effort alone.

### The Convergence

Neil becomes a light-seeker - following angels during visitations to catch a glimpse of Heaven's light. Incredibly dangerous (most die and go to Hell). During Barakiel's visitation, Neil crashes his truck near Janice and Ethan's campsite. Pinned, bleeding out from severed femoral artery.

Janice runs to help. A stray shaft of Heaven's light strikes her - makes her eyeless (like Benny). Then, as Neil is dying, a second shaft strikes him.

**What Heaven's light does to Neil:**
- Unmakes his eyes
- Reveals "all the reasons he should love God"
- He loves God "with an utterness beyond what humans can experience for one another"
- The grief, Sarah's suffering, his own pain - he's grateful for all of it, sees it as gift
- "Everything in life is love, even pain, especially pain"
- He dies truly devout, worthy of salvation

**What God does:**
Sends him to Hell anyway.

**No reason.** Neil didn't do anything wrong. There was no higher purpose. God just... didn't accept him.

### Neil in Hell

The most devastating passage:

> "For Neil, however, Hell bears no resemblance whatsoever to the mortal plane. [...] Just as seeing Heaven's light gave him an awareness of God's presence in all things in the mortal plane, so it has made him aware of God's absence in all things in Hell. Everything Neil sees, hears, or touches causes him distress, and unlike in the mortal plane this pain is not a form of God's love, but a consequence of His absence."

> "Neil still loves Sarah, and misses her as much as he ever did, and the knowledge that he came so close to rejoining her only makes it worse. He knows his being sent to Hell was not a result of anything he did; he knows there was no reason for it, no higher purpose being served. **None of this diminishes his love for God.**"

> "Neil even knows that by being beyond God's awareness, he is not loved by God in return. This doesn't affect his feelings either, because **unconditional love asks nothing, not even that it be returned.**"

> "And though it's been many years that he has in Hell, beyond the awareness of God, he loves Him still. **That is the nature of true devotion.**"

### Ethan's Ministry

Ethan witnesses all of this - sees Neil transformed by Heaven's light, sees his soul rise toward Heaven then descend to Hell. He becomes a preacher with a brutal message:

**"God is not just, God is not kind, God is not merciful, and understanding that is essential to true devotion."**

Love God anyway. Not under a misapprehension. Don't expect justice in the afterlife any more than in the mortal plane. If you wish to love God, be prepared to do so no matter what His intentions.

Neil's lost soul is the embodiment of these teachings.

## Theological Architecture - Final Assessment

Chiang took the premise seriously and followed it to its most brutal conclusion:

- **True devotion is unconditional** - even if God is arbitrary, capricious, unjust
- **Seeing Heaven's light rewires you** - you become genuinely devout, it's not fake
- **And it still doesn't matter** - God can still reject you for no reason
- **This is the point** - true love expects nothing in return, not even acknowledgment

It's nihilism wearing the costume of theology. Or maybe it's the most honest theology possible: if God exists and is all-powerful, He owes you nothing. Not justice, not mercy, not even the courtesy of consistency.

The gut-punch is that Neil GETS what he wanted (unconditional love of God, certainty that removes all doubt) and it makes his damnation WORSE, not better. He experiences more anguish in Hell than was possible when alive, precisely because Heaven's light gave him the capacity to feel God's absence so acutely.

## "Liking What You See: A Documentary"

Completely different format - presented as interview transcripts. The style shift is jarring but effective.

**The technology:** Calliagnosia ("calli") - neural modification that blocks the pathways that evaluate facial beauty. You still see faces perfectly (can distinguish features), you just don't experience aesthetic reactions to them. No "beautiful" or "ugly" - just faces.

**The setting:** Pembleton University. Students for Equality Everywhere (SEE) are sponsoring an initiative to make calliagnosia a requirement for all enrolled students. Adding it to the student-created Code of Ethical Conduct.

**The controversy:** 
- Tamera Lyons - grew up with calli at Saybrook School (calli-required private school), turns 18 and wants it OFF, feels "scammed" that Pembleton might require it
- Maria deSouza (SEE president) - sees calli as combating "lookism," calls it "assisted maturity"
- Parents at Saybrook chose it to protect kids from appearance-based judgment

**The science (from neurologist Joseph Weingartner):**
Absolute markers of beauty exist across cultures:
- Clear skin (health indicator, like bright plumage)
- Symmetry (resistance to environmental stressors during development)
- Facial proportions near population mean (genetic health)
- Departures from mean caused by sex hormones (reproductive potential)

Calliagnosia blocks response to these innate markers. Doesn't eliminate cultural beauty standards, but removes the biological predisposition. "Evens up the odds."

**The Saybrook philosophy:**
Rachel Lyons (Tamera's mother) saw girls with facial abnormalities thriving there - burn victims elected class president, comfortable and popular. Wanted daughter to value herself for what she could DO, not how she looked. "Being pretty is fundamentally a passive quality."

Martin Lyons (father): "There's more than enough stress involved in simply getting through adolescence; the peer pressure can crush you like a paper cup." Calli removes one vector of that pressure.

But they also respect that at 18, Tamera has the right to choose. Some people are ready at 16, some not until 30. You trust your child and hope for the best.

**Tamera gets it turned off:** Described some faces as "glowing" or "more vivid." The nurse showed her test results - pupil dilation, skin conductivity - spiked for beautiful faces. Says she'll notice others' faces right away, but take a while to have reactions to her own (too used to it).

The story cut off mid-sentence - she's looking in a mirror for the first time post-calli.

## Thematic Link

Both stories explore the relationship between choice and coercion around fundamental aspects of identity:

**"Hell is the Absence of God":** Can you choose to love God? Is forced devotion (via Heaven's light) still meaningful? Does it matter if it's "real" if the outcome is the same?

**"Liking What You See":** Can you choose not to value beauty when your brain is hardwired to respond to it? Is blocking those neural pathways liberation or deprivation? Who gets to decide - parents, society, individuals?

Both wrestle with what it means to be truly free when your brain/soul is shaped by forces beyond your control.
