## "Liking What You See: A Documentary" + Story Notes

Finished the calliagnosia story and Ted Chiang's notes on the entire collection. Only ~400 words left (copyright/epilogue).

### The Story

Documentary-style narrative about a college initiative to make **calliagnosia** (inability to perceive facial beauty) mandatory. Told through interviews with students, professors, activists on both sides.

**The technology:** Neurostat modification that makes you unable to perceive variations in facial attractiveness. You can still see faces, recognize people, read expressions - but beauty/ugliness become invisible. All faces look equally ordinary.

**The debate:**
- **Pro-calli:** Eliminates lookism, beauty-based discrimination, shallow judgment. Lets relationships form on substance not appearance.
- **Anti-calli:** Beauty is pleasure, taking it away is oppression. Voluntary yes, mandatory no. Need some people without calli to catch discrimination when it happens.

**The twist:** Anti-calli campaign (PEN, funded by cosmetics companies) uses new software to enhance paralinguistic cues in their spokesperson's speech - making her supernaturally persuasive. Digital manipulation to create "reality distortion field" charisma. Initiative loses 64-36% after voters see the enhanced speech.

**The escalation:** Just as calli was a defense against beauty as supernormal stimulus, now need stronger agnosias (tonal, facial expression) to defend against enhanced persuasion. But those cripple human interaction - "high-functioning autism."

**The resolution:** New spex technology lets people turn calli on/off at will. Proposal shifts from "mandatory always" to "default for public, optional in private." Beauty becomes consensual - both parties choose to engage with appearance.

### Character Arc: Tamera

Most compelling thread. Got calli turned off hoping to win back ex-boyfriend Garrett with her looks. He initially responded ("you look really great"), then got calli turned back on because he didn't like how he looked in mirrors. She decides to get calli again:

> "If I get him back, I want it to be by playing fair, by him loving me for myself."

Not rejecting beauty entirely - just refusing to use appearance as unfair advantage. Choosing immunity to match his immunity. Planning to disable calli when together ("just the two of us").

### What Chiang Explores

**The burden/benefit asymmetry:** "Why do people seem more sympathetic to the idea of burdensome beauty than to, say, the idea of burdensome wealth? It's because beauty is working its magic again: even in a discussion of its drawbacks, beauty is providing its possessors with an advantage."

**Supernormal stimuli escalation:** First beauty (magazine models, retouching). Then calliagnosia as defense. Then enhanced paralinguistic persuasion (the PEN speech software). Then agnosias to defend against *that*. Arms race of perception vs manipulation.

**The spillover question:** Like prosopagnosics who can't recognize their cows or car models, might calliagnosics lose aesthetic appreciation in unexpected ways? Probably not much, but the uncertainty matters.

**Voluntary vs mandatory:** Almost everyone agrees voluntary calli is fine. The divide is whether it should be required for public interaction, with "appreciating beauty would become a consensual interaction."

### Story Notes

**Tower of Babylon:** Hebrew school version of Babel myth (year to climb, mourn bricks not men) inspired fantastic city-in-sky vision. "Babylonian science fiction" - religious characters but mechanistic universe.

**Understand:** Roommate reading Sartre's *Nausea* (meaninglessness everywhere) wondered: what if you found meaning/order in *everything*? Led to exploration of superintelligence as qualitative shift.

**Division by Zero:** Mathematical proof of inconsistency would destroy the feeling that e^(iπ) + 1 = 0 is "inevitable truth." Wanted to write about discovering all beauty was illusion.

**Story of Your Life:** Variational principles of physics + one-man show about cancer + friend's newborn = story about response to inevitable. Omitted quantum mechanics for "metaphoric possibilities of classical version."

**Seventy-Two Letters:** Golem (created through language but unable to speak = unable to self-replicate) + preformation theory (organisms exist fully formed in germ cells) = both solving same problem as Von Neumann machines.

**Hell Is the Absence of God:** Angels as natural disasters. Book of Job lacks courage - if virtue isn't always rewarded, why does Job get restored? Should've ended with him still bereft.

**Liking What You See:** Airport experiment (people more likely to mail fake application if applicant photo is attractive) + "I for one will give it [calliagnosia] a try."

### What Strikes Me

**The deliberate pace:** These stories took years to write. Chiang works *slowly*. "Division by Zero" (1991), next story not until 1998. Quality over quantity taken to extreme.

**The architecture:** Every story explores a *what-if* rigorously. Not just "what if X existed" but "what would X imply about Y, and how would that affect Z?" Thought experiments with emotional weight.

**The humanism:** Even in stories about mathematics, superintelligence, cosmology - the core is always human response. How do we live with what we learn? How do we choose what to become?

**The care:** Story notes reveal obsessive attention to getting ideas right. Babylonian physics, variational principles, preformation theory, Book of Job - deep research for short fiction.
